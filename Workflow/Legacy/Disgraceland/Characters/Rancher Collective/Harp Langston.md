## Biography

><h4>"A man's only got two choices in this world. Work the land… or get buried in it."</h4>

**Harp Langston** is the reason Threshton didn’t die with the ash. Founder of the [[THRESHTON#Red Night Ranch|Red Night Ranch]] and the first to strike a devil’s bargain with [[The Duke]], Harp is legend stitched to leather. No one really knows how old he is — some say he came from [[The Drownlands]] on a dying mare with two rifles and a waxcloth pouch of seeds. Others say he built his ranch on a battlefield, where the soil still coughs up bullets and bones when the wind shifts.

What *is* known is that when the skies turned black and the hunger set in, Harp planted. While everyone else bartered, begged, or burned, Harp was putting posts in the dirt and cursing the land into submission. Against all odds, the earth listened. He brought life back to a place that had forgotten what green looked like. And when The Duke came crawling in with his false promises and trade offers, Harp made the first deal — not because he trusted him, but because he *understood* him. That deal built the foundation of the Rancher Collective.

Time, though, is a bastard. Harp’s hair is thinning now, his spine bent from a lifetime of labor. His knife is older than some townships, and twice as cruel. He doesn’t trust the other ranchers — especially not slick-talking [[Beau DuNoir]] or that worm-tongued rat [[Danny Dandelion]] (whose name he refuses to speak aloud). [[Freddy Blanchard]] is too noble for his liking. Only [[Turner Anderson]] gets a nod now and then — but even that’s rare, and never warm.

He runs Red Night like a war camp. No drinking, no music, no visitors without scars. The land produces more wheat than any other spread in the region, but no one calls it pleasant. You don’t go to Red Night for comfort. You go to survive.

His one living child, Mayor [[Soria Langston-Clay]] of [[Ashmoor]], is a bitter stain on his legacy. She betrayed the people of [[Threshton]] for power and [[The Duke]]’s favor — and Harp has never once spoken her name since. Was an old friend of legend, [[Darrell Douglass]]

When Harp speaks at a meeting, the others listen. They may not like him. Hell, most days they don’t. But they damn sure *respect* him. And if the day comes when Threshton needs to burn it all down and start over, you can bet Harp Langston’s the one holding the match.

## Personality

Harp is mean as rust and twice as unyielding. He’s not just gruff — he’s *hostile* to most people who breathe too loud or ask too many questions. He wakes before dawn, eats cold oats from a tin, and patrols his land like a soldier walking a trench. He despises snakes, lies, and ceremony. If someone wastes his time, they don’t get a second chance. The only music he tolerates is the sound of a hammer on steel or boots crunching dirt. Despite it all, he’s honest — brutally so — and demands the same from anyone under his roof.

## Role In Disgraceland
Harp Langston is the spine of the Rancher Collective — the unbreakable elder who remembers what Threshton was *before* the Duke, and who never stopped working to keep it alive. Every rancher in the Collective owes their foothold to Harp’s first gamble with survival. He’s not a leader in the traditional sense — he’s a relic. A hard, bitter man who still gets things done. If Threshton is a house built on dust and blood, Harp is the post that refuses to rot.

## Relationships
> ### [[Darrell Douglass]] 
> Of all the allies Harp Langston ever had, none ran deeper than **[[Darrell Douglass]]**. The two met in 2151, when Darrell managed to restart the old mill in [[Steeltown]] — a miracle by post-war standards. Harp rode up with a bag of dried oats and left with a bond. For years, they coordinated in the shadows, running the Black Route — a quiet supply chain of food, tools, and hope between Steeltown and Threshton. It was never official. No documents, no promises. Just trust. Harp didn’t trust easily, but he trusted Darrell. 
> That trust was tested in 2158 when Darrell was captured and forced to work under the Duke’s boot. Harp wanted to fight back, to rescue him, but the other ranchers refused to risk the Collective. It was one of the few times Harp let others outvote him. He and Darrell argued before the silence took hold. Harp buried the guilt under work, never speaking Darrell’s name in public again. But on the night Darrell was executed in 2167, something changed. A silo on the edge of Red Night Ranch caught fire, no livestock harmed. Just ash. A symbol left in the dirt: the same one Darrell wore on his arm. Harp never explained it. He didn’t need to.
